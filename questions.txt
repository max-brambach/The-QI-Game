Q1: You image a fluorescent sample with a 60x/1.4 NA objective (emission 510nm). According to the Nyquist criterion, what is the maximum pixel size you should use at the camera?

❌ A) 510nm, one wavelength per pixel
✅ B) ~90nm in sample space
❌ C) ~222nm, the approximate PSF FWHM
❌ D) As small as possible

Explanation: Rayleigh lateral resolution = 0.61*510/1.4) ≈ 222nm. Nyquist requires sampling at ≥2x the highest frequency, so pixel ~90nm in sample space. Undersampling aliases structure; oversampling wastes signal and increases bleaching.

Q2: When writing a paper, which details about your objective lens do you need to report?
❌ A) Serial number
✅ B) Manufacturer, correction class, magnification, NA, and immersion medium
❌ C) Magnification and focus settings
❌ D) Magnification only — it determines resolution
Explanation: Magnification alone is nearly meaningless for reproducibility — two 60× objectives can have vastly different optical performance. NA determines resolution and light collection, immersion medium must match your sample mounting, and manufacturer and correction class let another lab source the exact same optic. Serial number is too much; focus settings are an acquisition detail, not a lens property.


Q3: You fit a Gaussian to a single fluorescent bead and measure the PSF width. Would a dimmer bead give a different fitted PSF width even if the optics are identical?
❌ A) No — the PSF is a property of the optics alone, so bead brightness is irrelevant
❌ B) No — a dimmer bead would give a narrower PSF because fewer photons spread less
✅ C) Not necessarily wider or narrower, but less precisely determined — fewer photons mean noisier intensity values and a less reliable Gaussian fit
❌ D) Yes, always wider — dimmer beads emit lower energy photons which diffract more strongly at the objective
Explanation: The true PSF is a property of the optics, not the fluorophore. But measuring it requires fitting a model to noisy data, and noise is shot-noise limited — scaling with √N photons. With fewer photons, the low-intensity rings of the PSF are buried in noise and the Gaussian fit becomes uncertain. The estimated width could land high or low — what decreases is your confidence in the measurement. This is the same principle underlying SMLM: more photons don't change the PSF, they let you characterize it more precisely.
Q4: You choose a pixel dwell time of 4µs on a confocal with a 63× objective (NA 1.4, 488nm). You need a new frame every 5 seconds. Using Nyquist-sampled pixels, what is the maximum square FOV you can image?
❌ A) 256×256 pixels (~22×22µm)
❌ B) 512×512 pixels (~44×44µm)
✅ C) 1024×1024 pixels (~89×89µm)
❌ D) 2048×2048 pixels (~178×178µm)
Explanation: First calculate the maximum number of pixels: 5,000,000µs / 4µs = 1,250,000 pixels. The largest square grid that fits is 1024×1024 (1,048,576 pixels) — 2048×2048 would require 16.7 seconds. Then convert to physical size: Nyquist pixel size = (λ/2NA)/2 = (488/2×1.4)/2 ≈ 87nm. So 1024 × 87nm ≈ 89µm per side. Both steps are necessary — pixel budget sets the grid size, Nyquist converts it to physical dimensions.

Question: You increase laser power but fluorescence intensity plateaus but the detector is not saturating. What is the most likely explanation?
❌ A) Photobleaching
✅ B) Ground state depletion
❌ C) Dirty objective
❌ D) Stimulated emission
Explanation: Fluorescence requires a fluorophore in the ground state to absorb a photon. At high laser powers, molecules are re-excited so rapidly that most of the population is trapped in the excited or triplet state — the ground state is depleted. With fewer molecules available to absorb, additional laser power yields no additional signal. Detector saturation is the tempting wrong answer, but can be ruled out by checking the raw pixel values.

Question: When should you use rolling ball background subtraction instead of uniform background subtraction?
❌ A) When your signal is very dim and you need to preserve as many photons as possible
❌ B) When your background is uniformly low across the entire field
✅ C) When background intensity varies spatially across the image, such as from autofluorescence gradients
❌ D) When your cells are densely packed and leave no empty regions to sample background from


Explanation: Uniform background subtraction assumes a single background value applies everywhere, valid when illumination is flat. Rolling ball fits a local estimate at every position, capturing slow spatial variation across the field. If your background is spatially uniform, use uniform subtraction; if it varies due to autofluorescence gradients, use rolling ball.

Question: Which of the following correctly describes Manders' colocalization coefficients M1 and M2?

❌ A) M1 and M2 measure the Pearson correlation between channel intensities
✅ B) M1 is the fraction of channel 1 signal overlapping channel 2; M2 is the reverse
❌ C) M1 and M2 together give a p-value for colocalization significance
❌ D) M1=M2=1 means the two channels are anti-correlated

Explanation: Manders' coefficients are overlap fractions, not correlations. M1 = (Ch1 intensity where Ch2 > threshold) / (total Ch1 intensity). It answers: what fraction of protein A signal is found where protein B is present? M1 and M2 are asymmetric — A mostly with B (M1≈1) while B mostly elsewhere (M2≈0.1). Always apply intensity thresholds (Costes' method) before computing Manders'.

Question: In light sheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM), the illumination and detection objectives are perpendicular. What is the advantage over widefield or confocal?

✅ A) Only the focal plane is illuminated; far less bleaching across the sample volume
❌ B) Light sheet systems are simpler and less expensive than widefield
❌ C) The 90-degree geometry eliminates the need for fluorescent labels
❌ D) Higher lateral resolution than confocal thanks to the thin sheet

Explanation: Selective plane illumination! In widefield and confocal, every Z-plane gets illuminated every time you image. In LSFM, a thin sheet illuminates only the focal plane — everything else stays dark and unbleached. For live imaging of developing embryos or long time-lapses, this reduces total phototoxic dose compared to confocal.

Airyscan offers up to a root 2 improvement in resolution by incorporating a 32 PMT array detector and pixel reassignment. What is the core idea behind this strategy?

Question: You train a U-Net classifier to detect mitotic cells. Your model achieves 95% accuracy on a dataset where only 2% of cells are mitotic. What statement is true?

❌ A) Neural networks are imprecise and should not be used for cell classification
✅ B) A model predicting 'never mitotic' would score 98%. You need precision/recall or F1
❌ C) Suspicious. 95% accuracy seems too good for noisy biological data
❌ D) We are calling true mitotic cells with 95% accuracy. 

Explanation: Class imbalance! When positives are rare (2% of cells mitotic), a trivial model that always predicts negative scores 98% accuracy. Use precision (what fraction of predicted positives are real), recall (what fraction of real positives are found), F1-score, or AUROC. In biology, false negatives and false positives often have very different costs — choose your metric accordingly.

Question: You're acquiring a tiled image (10x10 grid) of a large tissue section and notice intensity differences at tile edges. What is the most likely cause?

Question: Your lab switches from a bicarbonate-buffered medium to HEPES-buffered medium for a live-cell experiment. What does this change about your environmental control requirements at the stage?
✅ A) You no longer need CO₂ control. HEPES buffers pH independently of CO₂
❌ B) You need more CO₂ to compensate for HEPES's lower buffering capacity
❌ C) HEPES requires higher temperature (39°C) to buffer effectively
❌ D) CO₂ is required to maintain the appropriate osmolarity


Explanation: Standard bicarbonate-buffered media maintain pH only in equilibrium with ~5% CO₂ — disrupt that and pH drifts fast. HEPES is a zwitterionic organic buffer that holds pH independently of CO₂, making it ideal for open-stage imaging. The tradeoff is that it's not great for long-term culture, so it's best reserved for shorter sessions. Temperature control remains essential either way.

Question: You want to image three fluorophores simultaneously: DAPI, GFP, and mCherry. What is a critical consideration when selecting emission filters?

❌ A) Choose the widest bandpass filters possible to maximize signal
✅ B) Ensure emission filters are narrow enough to minimize spectral bleedthrough 
❌ C) Always use longpass filters to collect more total photons
❌ D) Filter choice doesn't matter if laser power is low enough

Explanation: Spectral bleedthrough is the enemy of quantitative multichannel imaging! DAPI, GFP, and mCherry have overlapping emission tails, so narrow bandpass filters centred on each peak are essential to minimize bleedthrough. Wider filters collect more photons but also more emission from neighbouring fluorophores.
